How I work

Product, but make it theatre, lab and coven.

My practice lives at the intersection of rigorous discovery, clear communication and a bit of dramaturgy. I like to bring people into the process — not just the solution — so that decisions feel shared and grounded.

I treat discovery as an ongoing practice, not a phase. That means continuously talking to users, mapping signals and adjusting hypotheses — especially in environments where everything changes all the time.

Storytelling for alignment

I use narrative, visuals and prototypes to help teams see the same picture — and feel the consequences of decisions. Cases, journeys and anti-cases are part of how I bring strategy down to earth.

Care for the humans in the system

I look at users, teams and stakeholders as humans with constraints and emotions, not just roles. My background in theatre and tarot shows up as empathy, presence and the ability to hold ambiguity with care.

Grounded experimentation

I enjoy prototyping paths quickly, but always anchored in clear success metrics, guardrails and risk assessment — especially when dealing with money, health or learning outcomes.

ANTI-CASE · WHEN A GOOD PRODUCT ISN’T ENOUGH

Medway Academy: a robust, validated product that didn’t “win” — and what stayed with me.

Medway Academy was a bold bet: a new product for Brazilian medical students in the clinical cycle, built inside a company whose core ICP lives a few years ahead, in the internship phase. We built something innovative, loved by users and backed by a strong discovery practice — and still, the product was sunsetted.

“This is not a failure story. It’s the story of how a solid product can be sunsetted — and still leave deep value behind.”

11 in-depth interviews
300k BRL/month saved
3–4 MVPs validated
Quick timeline
  • 2022 · Idea & early signals
  • 2023 · Robust discovery & MVPs
  • 2024 · Product sunset & learnings
My role

Lead product designer for Medway Academy, working in a dedicated squad (PM, backend, frontend) and collaborating with content, marketing and leadership as they had capacity.

Team setup
  • Dedicated squad: PM, PD, backend, frontend.
  • Content, marketing and ops acting as satellites.
  • Leadership involved in key decisions and bets.

Context · Why this product, in this company.

Medway is an EdTech focused on helping Brazilian medical students pass residency exams — especially those in the final years of medical school (the internship). Medway Academy emerged as a bet to support students earlier in their journey, during the clinical cycle, and position the brand as a reference sooner.

The idea: offer an experience tailored to the daily reality of clinical cycle students, using cases, flashcards, questions and videos — all stitched together into study paths that respected their time, energy and cognitive load.

This meant building a new product inside an organisation whose core structure, metrics and brand positioning were still strongly tied to the internship ICP.

Discovery · A robust, well-structured process.

We ran a structured discovery to deeply understand the pains, rhythms and goals of clinical cycle students. This included qualitative interviews, diary-like sessions and multiple rounds of concept testing with prototypes.

We mapped key problems: overload, lack of structure, fragmentation between college content and exam prep, emotional fatigue. From there, we explored different ways to orchestrate content and rituals that could make daily study lighter and more sustainable.

Multi-method discovery

Interviews, usability tests and opportunistic feedback inside the product, connecting signals over time.

Continuous validation

Fast, low-cost MVPs to validate concepts before investing in full development.

Shared understanding

Discovery artefacts that helped leadership and teams see the ICP and problem space clearly.

Building · What we absolutely nailed.

We designed and shipped an innovative product that combined clinical cases, flashcards, multiple-choice questions and videos into structured study journeys. The experience leaned on gamification, streaks and ranking, but always with an eye on emotional sustainability.

Behind the scenes, we created an automated content production system that drastically reduced manual work and made the product scalable and sustainable for the content team.

The product’s core value proposition was validated with the right students, using a continuous discovery process that stayed alive despite organisational turbulence.
Validated concept

Strong qualitative signals of engagement, retention and perceived quality from clinical cycle students.

Automated content pipeline

An automated, scalable content system that saved ~300k BRL/month during the product’s lifetime.

Discovery muscle

A replicable, continuous discovery practice that could be reused by other teams and products.

Sunset · When a good product still doesn’t stay.

Even with a validated concept, strong user signals and a solid content engine, Medway Academy was sunsetted. The main reason: strategic focus and organisational constraints, not product quality.

The company needed to concentrate efforts on the core ICP and products that were closer to the existing brand, sales and operational structure. Supporting a parallel product with a different ICP, positioning and go-to-market proved too costly at that moment.

What stayed: an innovative product, a continuous discovery practice, a scalable content system, and a shared understanding of a new ICP that can inform future bets.

This experience reinforced a key lesson for me: a product can be “right” for users and still not be “right” for the organisation at a given time. As designers and product people, our job is also to understand and work with that tension.

What remained useful

The content engine, discovery patterns and ICP learnings are likely to be reused by core products.

What I carry forward

Practices like zero-cost MVPs, continuous discovery and clear storytelling around trade-offs.

What I now watch for

Alignment between product bets, organisational timing, ICP and the actual capacity to support them.
